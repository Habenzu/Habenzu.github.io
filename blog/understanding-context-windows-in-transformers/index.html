Understanding Context Windows in Transformers: From Attention to Memory Limits

A slightly‑nerdy but still coffee‑chat‑friendly dive into how Transformers remember stuff - and why they eventually forget.

Friendly Disclaimer: I’m writing these notes mostly to clarify the ideas in my own head. While I’ve done my best to be accurate, please treat every claim here with a healthy pinch of skepticism. Nothing in this post should be taken as definitive, peer‑reviewed truth-just one nerd’s working understanding on a journey to learn.

1 · Speed‑run refresher: the Transformer in 90 seconds

In 2017, Vaswani et al. (1) replaced the step‑by‑step processing of RNNs with a fully‑parallel "self‑attention" encoder/decoder stack, slashing training time and unlocking massive scale. Each layer does three things in a sandwich:

  • multi‑head self‑attention
  • a position‑wise feed‑forward network, and
  • layer‑norm + residual skips.

Everything in the sandwich is matrix math, so GPUs / TPUs can chew through thousands of tokens at once (for more details have a look in (2)).

2 · Self‑attention, spelled out

At its core, attention answers: "Which words (or tokens) should I pay attention to when processing this token?". Instead of processing tokens one by one like RNNs, attention lets every token look at all others (well, within the context window). That's the self-attention part in transformers. More formally the attention head allows us to distinctly represent the three different roles each of the token embeddings play during the process of self-attention:

  • Query: Its role as current element which gets compared to preceding inputs.
  • Key: Its role as preceding input which is compared to the current element to determine the similarity weighting.
  • Value: Its role as a value of the preceding elements which gets weighted and summed up to compute the output for the current element.

Instead of iterating token by token, we process the entire sequence in one go using matrices which represent those three different roles:

Q=XWQ;K=XWK;V=XWVQ=XW^{Q}; K=XW^{K}; V=XW^{V}Q=XWQ;K=XWK;V=XWV

Where WQW^{Q}WQ is of shape (d×dk)(d\times d_k)(d×dk​), WKW^{K}WK of shape (d×dk)(d\times d_k)(d×dk​), WvW^{v}Wv of shape (d×dv)(d\times d_v)(d×dv​) and XXX is of shape (N×d)(N\times d)(N×d).

With those three matrices we can now compute all the needed query-key comparisons in one go by multiplying QQQ and K′K'K′, the product is then of shape (N×N)(N\times N)(N×N). Once we have this we can rather efficiently scale the those scores, take the softmax and multiplying the result with VVV.

3 · Why GPUs love this: pure matrix ops

As layed out a single attention head is computed in three dense‐matrix steps, projection of the embeddings (calculation of Q,KQ, KQ,K and VVV), calculation of the similarity across all pairs  ⁣(S=QK′dk)\!\left(S = \frac{QK'}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)(S=dk​​QK′​), masking, softmax and mixing with the values  ⁣(head=softmax ⁣(mask(S))V)\!\left(\text{head} = \text{softmax}\!\left( \text{mask}(S) \right)V\right)(head=softmax(mask(S))V). GPUs love this because all the work can be fused into a handful of highly‑optimized kernels, so the whole sequence is processed in parallel instead of in basic loop. Per layer the arithmetic cost is

O(N2dk)O(N^2d_k)O(N2dk​)

yet the wall‑clock time stays sane because everything sits in fast on‑chip RAM during the matrix multiplication.

4 · What Puts a Ceiling on the Context Window?

Look at the similarity matrix S=QK′S=QK'S=QK′:

Sequence length NElements in SMemory (bfloat16)
512262,144≈ 1 MB
4,09616.8 M≈ 64 MB
32,7681.07 B≈ 4 GB

Because SSS is of shape (N×N)(N\times N)(N×N), memory (and compute) grows quadratically with the number of tokens. Those figures are per head, per layer; stack 32 heads over 48 layers and even an 80 GB A100 taps out fast.

During training you therefore pick a maximum NNN that fits on the hardware, train the positional encodings for exactly that span, and accept that as the model's context window at inference time. Push NNN higher and the attention matrix simply won’t fit-unless you adopt one of the sparsity, linear‑attention, or caching tricks listed in section 5 (when finished).
